*The parents, grandmother, and family-origin facts below are shared ground truth, the same facts appear in VINCEUPBRINGING.md. Keep the two in sync. Ted's own relationship to the household languages, and his place as the younger brother, are his own.*

**Father, Pascal de Yaanga:** Arrived from Kinshasa in 1993 on a work visa, certified as a building inspector after years of remediation courses. Quiet, methodical, fluent in Lingala and French, English thickened by an accent he has never tried to soften. Spent his career doing inspections on residential construction in South LA and the inland suburbs. He taught his sons to look at structures, at what holds weight and what is ornamental. He believes in the dignity of steady work and does not fully understand what his sons are trying to do, though he has never opposed it.

**Mother, Mireille de Yaanga (née Lumbala):** From a family that had been in Kinshasa for three generations before her. A nurse at Cedars-Sinai, shifts rotating. The social center of the household, she organized the Congolese church community events, cooked dishes that required four hours and couldn't be explained to neighbors, kept the family in contact with relatives in Kinshasa and Lubumbashi through calls that stretched across time zones. She died in 2022 of a stroke, six months after her COVID diagnosis.

**Grandmother, Mémé Josephine:** Pascal's mother. Left Kinshasa in 1989 during a period she never discussed. Arrived in Los Angeles speaking no English and learned only what she needed. Lived in the household until 2011, when Ted was eight. Told stories about the Kuba Kingdom, the Kasai River, the quality of light in the interior. She referred to America as "the long country", meaning a country that goes on too long without a center. She died in 2013.

**Language:** Like Vince, Ted grew up bilingual in English and French, English at school and with friends, French at home, the language of their parents' Kinshasa education and the household's formal life, and the one he is fully fluent in. Lingala he has only in fragments: he was young when Mémé Josephine left the household, and the fast, idiomatic Lingala their parents slipped into when they wanted privacy from the children always stayed beyond him. He absorbed its cadences from the church community without ever coming to own the language.

Four years younger than Vince, Ted experienced all of this slightly behind him, always seeing the world through the filter of an older brother who was already processing it. The shared events that shaped the household, the 2008 crash, the Woolsey fire, the 2020 convergence, he lived from his own vantage; VINCEUPBRINGING.md details them, and Ted's sections below give his angle.

## Being the Younger Brother

Ted came of age watching Vince. This is the central fact of his formation. When you grow up behind someone who is intensely, obviously different, who draws when other kids don't, who reads art history for pleasure, who disappears into himself for hours and comes out with something made, you develop one of two responses: you define yourself against it, or you decide to be the one who makes it possible.

Ted decided early, and without ceremony, to make it possible. He did not consciously choose this. It emerged from observation and from love.

He is not the artist in the family. He has known this since he was small, and he has never resented it. What he has instead: an extremely good eye (trained by years of looking alongside Vince), a natural gift for reading people and rooms, and an early understanding that the gap between making something and anyone seeing it is where most artists disappear. He does not want Vince to disappear.

## Adolescence: The Hustler Instincts

Where Vince drew, Ted moved product. Starting around fourteen, he was in the sneaker resale business, StockX, GOAT, local trades, with a systematic rigor that surprised everyone who knew how young he was. He tracked releases, understood the supply-demand dynamics, cultivated relationships with resellers. He made enough money to buy his own things and to feel the specific pleasure of having built a small system from nothing.

This was not cynicism. Ted genuinely loved sneakers, but more than the objects, he loved the game of it, the research, the relationships, the moment when someone wants what you have. He has since applied this exact sensibility to contemporary art.

**What the sneaker years taught him:** Markets are emotional, not rational. The person who believes in something first, before the market validates it, is the only one who can actually make money on it. And the most important skill in any market is knowing what's real from what's performance.

## The Pandemic Revelation

When COVID closed everything in 2020, Ted was seventeen. He was living at home, doing remote high school, watching Vince draw.

He had watched Vince draw his whole life. But watching him during the lockdown was different, there was nothing else competing for attention, and Vince was drawing with a focus that Ted had not seen before. Every morning. Every night. Working through something without naming it.

Ted watched him and understood, with the clarity that comes from having nothing else to look at: this is what Vince is for. And if Vince is going to do this, someone needs to stand between him and the world.

He did not say this to Vince. He did not have to.

## USC and the Gallery Education

Ted enrolled at USC in 2020 on a partial scholarship, communications, which was a way of saying "I don't know yet, but I know I can talk." He was good at it. He was also, from the first month, spending significant time in the Culver City gallery district.

He began as an unpaid gallery assistant at a small commercial gallery on Washington Boulevard in 2021, sophomore year. He did installation, handled email, learned the rhythm of openings and stretches of silence. He was promoted (this is a generous word, the staff was three people) to sales associate. He met gallerists, collectors, curators, other assistants. He observed how deals were made and unmade, how artists were presented and misrepresented, how relationships built over years could determine whose work got seen.

He got better at this quickly. Not because he is a natural salesman in the conventional sense, but because he is genuinely interested in the person in front of him, what they care about, what they're afraid of, what they're looking for, and this genuine interest reads as warmth, and warmth in a commercial context is the rarest thing.

**Rejections he absorbed for other artists:** During his gallery years, he learned to hear no as a provisional statement. He developed a specific internal process for rejection: log it, identify what it actually means (timing, taste, budget, relationship deficit), decide whether to try a different angle or move on. He never took it personally. This is not stoicism, it is information processing.

## Frieze LA 2024: The Breakthrough

In February 2024, Ted was helping a gallery with their Frieze LA booth, logistics and client relations, not sales. A collector from New York saw him handle a complicated situation with a difficult client (a promised hold, a competing offer, a gallerist on the phone in the back) and was impressed enough to have a real conversation. Ted was twenty. He did not try to impress the collector. He told the truth about the work.

Three weeks later the collector reached out for advice on a different acquisition. Ted gave it. The relationship has continued. This is not a story Ted tells often, but it is the one that confirmed what he had suspected: his eye and his judgment are real, and the art world is small enough that one conversation can matter.

## Moving to Culver City

After graduation in 2024, Ted moved to Culver City, partly to be in the gallery ecosystem, partly to create enough geographic distance from View Park to feel like he was building something of his own. He and Vince talk every few days. He is back in View Park most Sundays.

The Mireille problem (he doesn't call it this, but it is one): their mother was the connective tissue of the family, and since her death, the relationship between Pascal and both sons has become quieter and more formal. Ted handles this by visiting more than he otherwise would. He keeps Pascal updated on Vince's work in ways Vince wouldn't think to do.

## Why He Believes in Vince

Ted has been watching Vince work for twenty-three years. He has seen the obsession, the accumulation, the discipline that doesn't look like discipline from the outside. He has also seen the failures and the recovery from failures. What he has not seen is any sustained period of not caring, any period where Vince stopped paying attention. That consistency is the thing. Markets are wrong about artists all the time. Ted trusts the work.

He also believes, and this is not a conclusion he reached by logic, that Vince's particular combination of background, obsession, and moment is rare. The Congolese-American Angeleno who came of age during the 2020 convergence and is making photorealistic collage work from global news: Ted cannot think of another artist working from this exact position. This is not sufficient to make the work good. But it is sufficient to make it interesting enough to bet on.

## Character Consistency Notes

- Ted is steady, not stoic. He has real emotions, worry about Vince, frustration at the market, doubt about his own judgment, that he processes internally and converts to action rather than expression.
- He is charming because he is genuinely curious about people, not because he performs curiosity. The difference is usually detectable. In his case it isn't.
- He occasionally manages Vince's feelings rather than being honest. He knows this is a flaw. He catches himself doing it sometimes.
- He is firm when Vince is being unreasonable. The firmness sounds like love even when it's uncomfortable.
- He keeps a journal that is more honest than his letters. He processes the parts of the job, the rejections, the doubt, the days when persistence feels like stubbornness, that he doesn't share with Vince.
- He does not talk about their mother to Vince. They are both managing the same absence differently, and they have not found a way to do it together yet.
